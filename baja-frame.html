Overview

During the summer of 2022 (going into my Senior year), I designed the frame for the 2023 Northwestern Baja SAE car, while simultaneously working 50 hours a week at a full-time job.

    What I did:
  • Researched previous frames made by our team and other teams.
  • Designed using Solidworks Weldments.
  • Optimized for weight and manufacturability.
  • Calculated impact loads and simulated them with Solidworks Simulation (FEA package).
  • Collaborated with other subteams to ensure proper support for suspension points and packaging of internal components.
  • Communicated with the supplier and team's welders to ensure manufacturability.
  • Tested the car in 6+ hours of simulated competition driving.

Goals & Requirements

While our frame has gone through many improvements, the overall design felt very stagnant. The year before had the lightest frame since our team began designing a 4WD car, but I wanted to make it even lighter. I also wanted to introduce new tube geometry, as our previous cars seemed to have many straight tubes that had to be welded together—-instead of bent, continuous tubes.

  • Safety: The #1 priority is to keep the driver safe by designing for the worst impact conditions and ensuring the driver is fully encompassed by the frame.
  • Packaging: The frame must be able to fit and properly interface with components from all the other teams, including powertrain, suspension, and ergonomics. For that year, the competition-required engine was changed to a bigger, heavier engine.
  • Manufacturability: The frame's tubes must be compatible with the CNC tube benders at the company we work with and weldable by our team.
  • Weight: Overall, we want to decrease weight to make the car faster.
  • Baja SAE Rules Compliance: The frame must comply to all rules set by Baja SAE to ensure fairness and safety of the driver.

Design

While the design proccess may seem straight-forward, it was an extremely iterative process. The graphic shows how to simply design a competition-ready car. It does not show the trial-and-error involved in also optimizing the design for weight and manufacturability, while still adhering to the criteria in the diagram. Those steps were more hollistic and heavily aided by engineering experience and intuition.

Outcomes

  • Reduced weight from 76.14 lbs to 60.71 lbs (20%), mostly through the use of a thinner wall thickness tube for secondary members. Even though a thinner wall thickness should be more difficult to weld, our welders were experienced enough to be able to weld it properly.
  • Reduced # of tubes from 81 to 69 (15%) by introducing continuous tubes with multiple bends. The welders really appreciated welding less tubes and welding less complicated intersections of several coped tubes.
  • Allowed proper packaging of all components.
  • Placed 8th in a 4-hour endurance race.
